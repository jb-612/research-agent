# Discussion: Perioperative Tumor Secretome and Metastatic Progression

The perioperative period represents a critical, yet under-investigated, biological transition state that fundamentally reshapes the trajectory of metastatic disease. While traditional surgical oncology has focused on the mechanical removal of the primary tumor (PT) and the management of surgical stress, our findings suggest a paradigm shift: the abrupt loss of PT-derived systemic signals—the tumor secretome—is a primary driver of postoperative metastatic dynamics. This study demonstrates that early-stage metastatic lesions exhibit a stage-specific dependency on the PT secretome, and that perioperative targeting of key secretome components, specifically CCL5 and CXCL10, can reprogram the systemic immune landscape toward anti-tumor activity.

A central finding of this study is the transient regression of early metastatic lesions following PT excision. This observation aligns with previous reports of spontaneous metastatic regression in both preclinical models and clinical case studies (Ricon et al., 2020; Eckerling et al., 2023). We propose that metastatic maturation involves a transition from **secretome-dependency to autonomy**. Early micro-metastases, lacking a fully developed local niche, rely on systemic 'lifelines' provided by the PT secretome, including growth factors and immunosuppressive cytokines. As metastases progress to advanced stages, they likely acquire self-sufficiency in secreting these supportive factors, rendering them resistant to the systemic changes induced by PT removal. This stage-specific dependency highlights a 'window of vulnerability' in early metastatic progression that can be therapeutically exploited.

Our proteomic and cytokine profiling identified **CCL5 (RANTES)** and **CXCL10 (IP-10)** as pivotal mediators of this pro-tumorigenic secretome. While these chemokines are traditionally recognized for their role in recruiting cytotoxic T cells, their 'hijacking' by the tumor microenvironment is a well-documented phenomenon in aggressive malignancies (Karin, 2018; Aldinucci et al., 2020). In our models, high levels of PT-derived CCL5 and CXCL10 were associated with increased tumor cell proliferation and worse survival outcomes, a finding corroborated by our analysis of breast cancer patient cohorts. This dual role suggests that in the context of a high tumor burden, these chemokines may contribute to a 'smoke screen' effect—either by desensitizing their respective receptors (CCR5 and CXCR3) on effector cells through systemic saturation or by preferentially recruiting immunosuppressive populations such as MDSCs and regulatory T cells (Tregs).

Remarkably, the short-term perioperative blockade of CCR5 and CXCR3 using **Maraviroc** and **AMG 487** was sufficient to reverse this suppressive state. The observed increase in CD8+ T cell infiltration and the concurrent reduction in MDSCs and M1-to-M2 macrophage ratios suggest an **immune reset**. This is consistent with the work of Halama et al. (2016), who demonstrated that CCR5 antagonism can repolarize the immune microenvironment in colorectal cancer. In the perioperative context, where the immune system is already stressed by surgical trauma, these interventions may prevent the further 'tipping' of the balance toward metastasis. The fact that a transient (3-day) intervention yielded systemic immune alterations emphasizes the plasticity of the immune system during this critical window.

Furthermore, our results indicate that the tumor secretome acts as an ecosystem regulator beyond its direct effects on malignant cells. The observation that tumor-conditioned media (TCM) supports fibroblast proliferation and survival suggests that the PT secretome actively conditions the pre-metastatic niche. This stromal remodeling, mediated by factors such as CCL5 (which is known to activate cancer-associated fibroblasts), further facilitates the survival of disseminated tumor cells (DTCs) (Wightman et al., 2015). By disrupting this systemic communication, we not only starve the tumor cells of growth signals but also dismantle the supportive stromal framework they require.

Despite the promising findings, several limitations must be acknowledged. The use of syngeneic mouse models (B16F10 and 4T1), while immunocompetent, may not fully capture the complexity and heterogeneity of human cancer. Additionally, the transient nature of the observed regression suggests that secretome loss alone may not be curative for all patients, particularly those with more advanced, autonomous lesions. Future studies should focus on identifying combinatorial strategies that pair secretome blockade with standard-of-care immunotherapy or stress-reduction protocols (Hiller et al., 2020).

In conclusion, our study redefines the perioperative period as a dynamic biological transition. By identifying the tumor secretome as a critical regulator of early metastatic progression and immune evasion, we provide a biological rationale for targeted perioperative interventions. The CCL5/CCR5 and CXCL10/CXCR3 axes emerge as viable therapeutic targets that, if inhibited during the narrow window of surgical resection, may significantly improve long-term oncological outcomes.
