Abstract
Surgical resection of primary tumors is a life-saving approach, yet metastases remain a primary cause of cancer death. The perioperative period holds a delicate balance between pro- and anti- metastatic process and can determine long-term metastatic outcomes. While removal of the primary tumor (PT) eliminates a major source of tumor-secreted factors, the functional role of perioperative tumor secretome in regulating early metastatic progression remains poorly understood. Here, we investigate how PT excision and the consequent loss of tumor secretome affect metastatic progression in melanoma and breast cancer models. Using syngeneic B16F10 and 4T1 models, we show that PT removal induces a transient regression of early-stage metastatic lesions, whereas more advanced metastases are less affected, indicating stage-specific dependance on PT-derived factors. In vivo administration of tumor secretome enhanced tumor growth, supporting a pro-tumorigenic role for tumor secretome. In vitro, tumor secretome promoted tumor cell proliferation and survival in a tumor load-dependent manner and exerted similar pro-survival effects on non-malignant fibroblasts, highlighting its role in shaping the tumor microenvironment. Proteomic and cytokine profiling identified the chemokines CCL5 and CXCL10 as key secretome components related to metastatic potential. In vitro studies demonstrated that CXCL10 and CCL5 contribute to tumor cell proliferation, and in vivo perioperative blockade of their receptors reprogrammed the systemic immune landscape toward anti-tumor activity, characterized by increased CD8⁺ T cells and reduced immunosuppressive populations. Importantly, elevated PT levels of CCL5 and CXCL10 correlated with worse survival in preclinical models, and with reduced recurrence-free survival in breast cancer patients. Together, these findings identify tumor secretome as a critical regulator of perioperative metastatic progression and highlight chemokine signaling as a potential therapeutic target.

Keywords: Primary tumor secretome, metastasis, anti-tumor immunity, the perioperative period, CCL5-CCR5 signaling, CXCL10-CXCR3 signaling

Introduction

Surgical resection of primary tumors remains the cornerstone of curative treatment for most solid malignancies, yet postoperative metastases remain the primary cause of death. During the short perioperative period, lasting days to weeks before and after surgery, there is a balance between pro- and anti-metastatic processes, which may determine postoperative metastatic outcomes (Ref). In fact, ample evidence suggests that the perioperative period is highly influential in determining long-term cancer outcomes (Ref). On one hand, perioperative events such as tissue damage, use of anesthetic and analgesic agents, blood transfusions, hypothermia, nociception and pain, have all been shown to promote stress-inflammatory responses, including local and systemic release of catecholamines (CAs) and prostaglandins (PGs). These signaling pathways were shown to accelerate cancer progression and metastasis by suppressing anti-tumor immunity, by promoting angiogenesis, and by increasing tumor cells proliferation, survival, invasion, and immune evasion8. On the other hand, primary tumor (PT) excision eliminates the continuous dissemination of tumor cells into the circulation and distant tissues, abolishes PT-induced immune suppression, and terminates tumor secretion of pro-metastatic factors such as pro-angiogenic and growth factors11. Thus, removal of the PT disrupts a major source of tumor-derived factors, fundamentally altering systemic tumor-host interactions.
PTs secrete a variety of soluble factors, including cytokines, chemokines, and growth factors, as well as extracellular vesicles, collectively termed secretome(ref). Tumor secretome can support the metastatic cascade both at the primary tumor level and at distant organs19-23. Such milieu of secreted factors is rich in pro-angiogenic factors, growth factors, immune-suppressive factors, ECM degrading enzymes (MMPs), all of which can support both the formation of a pre-metastatic niche and the development of metastases19-21. While tumor secretome profiles were extensively studied in pre-clinical models and in vitro approaches24-27, most pre-clinical studies of the metastatic cascade do not include the clinically relevant resection of the primary tumor17. Thus, the dynamic contribution and significance of tumor secretome in the perioperative context of tumor removal remains poorly understood. Furthermore, as PT excision dramatically changes the systemic physiological landscape20, it is important to study the dynamics between PT secretome, PT excision, and metastatic growth. 
Importantly, metastatic dissemination often occurs early in disease progression, and undetectable micro-metastatic foci are likely present at the time of primary tumor excision, as supported by both experimental models and clinical observations28-3132-34. These early lesions, which are mostly undetectable clinically, may depend on systemic tumor-derived signals, and their abrupt loss following tumor resection may critically influence the fate of early metastatic lesions. However, how these lesions respond to the perioperative disruption of tumor-derived signaling remains largely unknown.

Emerging evidence suggests that local treatment of primary tumors can exert systemic effects on distant disease. There are numerous case studies that describe a spontaneous regression of metastases following PT excision15, 16. This is a rare but validated phenomenon in several cancer types41,42, that more commonly occurs in lung metastases15. These outcomes were reported in clinically detectable macro-metastases, but undetectable micro-metastases may be affected as well, perhaps even to a greater extent. Such effects can be immune-mediated, as described by “The abscopal effect”12, 13 , or induced by elimination of PT-secreted factors following PT removal. Indeed, we recently found in a xenograft breast cancer model that early-stage micro-metastases, which have spontaneously developed in the lungs and lymph-nodes, significantly regressed following PT excision into a dormant non-progressing state14. This metastatic regression was mediated by elimination of specific PT-secreted factors: SERPIN E1, MIF, IL-8 and PDGFaa, all of which are known for their pro-metastatic effects14. Importantly, macro-metastases were less affected by PT-excision, potentially as they become self-sufficient in secretion of growth-supporting factors, unlike micro-metastases. However, these findings were introduced in an immune-deficient model and should be tested in immune-competent models. Overall, these observations raise the possibility that perioperative disruption of PT-derived signaling may critically influence early metastatic dynamics, potentially through direct effects on tumor cells, as well as through modulation of anti-tumor immunity.
Here, we investigate in immune-competent animals how PT excision and the consequent loss of PT secretome regulate early metastatic progression in syngeneic melanoma and breast cancer models. We demonstrate that early-stage metastatic lesions exhibit a transient dependence on PT-derived factors, whereas more advanced lesions are less affected. Using in vivo and in vitro approaches, we show that tumor secretome promotes tumor cell survival and proliferation, and shapes the tumor microenvironment. Through integrative analyses, we identify the chemokines CCL5 and CXCL10 as key components of tumor secretome with pro-tumorigenic and immunomodulatory functions in the perioperative context. Furthermore, we show that targeting their signaling pathways enhances anti-tumor immunity and decreases tumor cell proliferation. Overall, we study the intricate relations between PT excision, elimination of pro-metastatic tumor- secreted factors, anti-tumor immunity, and metastatic growth, supporting a better understanding of the perioperative dynamics and impacts of tumor secretome. These insights may inform the design of studies assessing perioperative therapeutic strategies, supporting consideration of tumor-host signaling. 

Results

	•	Primary tumor removal induces transient regression of spontaneous early metastatic lesions
To examine the impact of primary tumor (PT) removal on metastatic progression, we used syngeneic B16F10 melanoma and 4T1 breast cancer models, in which spontaneous lymph node and lung metastases develop following tumor inoculation. PT excision was performed once metastatic burden reached a predefined bioluminescence imaging (BLI) threshold (see Methods), and metastatic progression was monitored along 14 days (Fig. 1A). 
In the B16F10 model, PT removal induced a rapid regression of metastatic BLI signal in animals bearing low metastatic burden at the time of PT excision, but not in in animals with higher baseline metastatic burden, measured one day following excision (Fig. 1B-D, Supplementary Fig. S1A-B). This size-dependent effect was statistically significant, and consistent across animals with low metastatic burden. Importantly, the regression observed in early-stage lesions was transient, lasting 2-7 days, after which metastatic growth resumed (Supplementary Fig. S1B).
These findings were replicated in the 4T1 breast cancer model (Fig. 1E, Supplementary Fig. S1C-D), demonstrating that early-stage metastases exhibit a transient dependence on the presence of the primary tumor.

	•	Tumor secretome promotes tumor growth in vivo and in vitro, especially at low tumor burden
We next assessed whether the observed dependence on the primary tumor is mediated by tumor-derived secreted factors. To this end, we studied the effects of tumor cell-conditioned medium (CM) in vivo and in vitro.
In the B16F10 model, continuous 7-day in vivo administration of CM (starting after tumor cell inoculation) significantly enhanced primary tumor growth as measured by bioluminescence imaging compared to the control group, which was administered with serum-free medium (SM) (Fig. 2A-B). This finding supports a pro-tumorigenic role for tumor-derived factors. Interestingly, despite CM administration lasting only 7 days, a marginally significant trend was also observed in PT volume reaching faster to a criterion of 100mm3 volume, measured more than three weeks after cessation of CM administration, suggesting long-lasting effects for tumor secretome (Fig. 2C). 
In vitro, CM promoted tumor cell proliferation and reduced apoptosis in both B16F10 and 4T1 cells (Fig. 2D-F, Supplementary Fig. 2A-D). Notably, the CM-promoting effects on proliferation in the B16F10 model were more pronounced at lower cell densities, indicating that tumor secretome preferentially supports tumor growth under low tumor burden conditions (Fig. 2D). 
To determine whether tumor secretome also affects non-malignant components of the tumor microenvironment, we examined its impact on fibroblasts proliferation and apoptosis. CM from both the 4T1 and B16F10 models significantly increased fibroblast proliferation and reduced apoptosis (Fig. 2G-I, Supplementary Fig. 2E-G), demonstrating that tumor-secreted factors support also stromal cells. In contrast to tumor cells, these effects were independent of initial cell density, suggesting distinct tumor secretome advantage for low cell density in malignant cells. 
Together, these findings indicate that tumor secretome promotes tumor progression in vivo and in vitro, both directly and potentially through modulation of the tumor microenvironment, with a preferential impact under conditions of low tumor burden.

	•	Primary tumor secretome composition predicts metastatic outcome 
To evaluate the predictive value of PT secretome for long-term metastatic outcome, we compared the proteomic profiles of tumor-conditioned media (TCM) derived from excised B16F10 tumors of animals that developed overt metastases or survived long after tumor excision (Fig. 3A). This analysis identified a distinct signature of tumor secreted proteins upregulated in tumors from long-term surviving animals, with pathway enrichment analysis indicating enhanced immune-related processes in survivors, potentially reflecting activation of anti-tumor immunity (Fig. 3B-C). These results suggest that the secretion profile of the PT can shape anti-tumor immunity to affect long-term metastatic outcomes.
To further explored the relationship between tumor secretome and metastatic potential, we used the low metastatic B16F1 and the highly metastatic B16F10 tumor cell-line variants. While primary tumor growth was comparable between the two lines, post-excision survival outcomes differed significantly (Supplementary Fig. 3A-B). Next, we compared the effects of B16F1 TCM (produced from excised PTs) to B16F10 TCM on B16F1 tumor cell proliferation. Both TCMs promoted B16F1 proliferation in a cell density-dependent manner. Importantly, the highly metastatic B16F10 TCM more effectively promoted B16F1 proliferation (Fig. 3D). We also assessed the effects of the B16F10 TCM on B16F10 tumor cell proliferation, and again identified cell-density dependent promotion of proliferation (Fig. 3E), replicating the effect of tumor-cell CM observed in Section 2 also using bulk tumor secretome. 

	•	Identifying CCL5 and CXCL10 as potential mediators of the pro-tumorigenic effects of tumor secretome

To identify specific PT-secreted factors which may mediate the observed pro-tumorigenic effects of tumor secretome, we compared 4 conditions using a cytokine array, in both the B16F10 and the 4T1 models: (i) tumor cells conditioned medium (CM) (in vitro); (ii) ex-vivo tumor bulk secretome (TCM); (iii) plasma from mice bearing a primary tumor, and (iv) plasma from naive control mice (Fig. 4A). We looked for secreted proteins that are present in both CM and TCM, as both were found to promote tumor growth and proliferation (see Sections 2-3). We also identified cytokines upregulated in plasma from PT-bearing mice relative to naïve mice, which could be secreted from the tumor. Additionally, we crossed our analysis with cytokine array data comparing B16F10 to B16F1 TCM, published by Magidey-Klein et al24. 
We identified 2 chemokines that were upregulated (above 1.5 fold-change) both in plasma from B16F10 PT-bearing mice relative to naïve mice, and in B16F10 relative to B16F1 TCM: CCL5 and CXCL10 (Fig. 4B-C, Supplementary table S1). In the 4T1 model, CCL5 and CXCL10 were also present in CM and in TCM, and CXCL10 was upregulated in plasma of PT-bearing relative to naïve mice (Supplementary table XX). 
According to the literature, these two chemokine pathways are hijacked by solid malignancies, including melanoma44, 45 and breast cancer(REFS), and are reported to be pro-metastatic or pro-tumorigenic44, 46, 47, but were not studied in the perioperative context. Thus, we considered CCL5 and CXCL10 as potential candidate mediators of tumor secretome pro-metastatic activity in the perioperative timeframe.

	•	Targeting CCL5 and CXCL10 modulates tumor growth and anti-tumor immunity

To assess the functional role of CCL5 and CXCL10, we evaluated the effects of their blockade on tumor cells proliferation in vitro and on anti-tumor immunity in vivo. 
As both cell-lines express the receptors for CCL5 and CXCL10, CCR5(refs) and CXCR3(refs), respectively, we first assessed the direct in vitro neutralization of either CCL5 or CXCL10 (using specific or isotype control antibodies), on tumor cells proliferation in tumor cells CM or SM as control. In B16F10 cells, CXCL10 blockade antagonized CM-induced proliferation back to baseline and even below-baseline levels, as well as reduced proliferation in all SM conditions (Fig. 5A). CCL5 blockade also antagonized CM-induced proliferation, but the effect was antibody dose-dependent (Fig. 5B). In the 4T1 model, no effects for CCL5 or CXCL10 blockade were found on tumor cells proliferation (Supplementary Fig. S4A-B), and the blockade did not alter apoptosis rates in any of the cell lines (Supplementary Fig. S4C-D).

In addition to the direct effects of CCL5 and CXCL10 on tumor cells, and given the established immunomodulatory roles of these chemokines, (ref) we next examined the effects of short-term in vivo perioperative blockade of their receptors on anti-tumor immunity. As each of their receptors (CCR5 for CCL5 and CXCR3 for CXCL10) can also bind other ligands (refs), we chose to systemically inhibit the receptors rather than the chemokines themselves. We used small molecule inhibitory drugs to block CXCR3 and CCR5 using AMG 487 and Maraviroc, respectively. We treated PT-bearing mice from both the B16F10 and the 4T1 models with either AMG 487, Maraviroc or with vehicle for 3 consecutive days, twice a day, followed by collection of blood, the PT, spleen and the lungs (Fig. 6A). As both CXCR3 and CCR5 are expressed on T cells and myeloid cells (refs), we measured CD8+ and CD4+ T cells, including regulatory T cells, MDSCs and macrophage cell populations in all harvested organs. We employed flow cytometry to examine distribution and activation of these immune cell populations both locally within the tumor, as well as systemically, including in the lungs which are a common metastatic site in both tumor models (refs). Of note, these drugs are usually used for prolonged periods of administration in the literature(refs), and not during the short perioperative period.
We found that both drugs improved anti-tumor immunity across both tumor models, upregulating CD8+ T cells, downregulating Tregs and MDSCs, and upregulating M1-like macrophages (Fig. 6, Supplementary Fig. S5). Maraviroc had more pronounced effects relative to AMG 487, but both drugs presented immune alterations related to positive prognosis in both the B16F10 and the 4T1 models. 

Detailed results:
In the 4T1 model, Maraviroc improved precentage of effector T cells over immune suppressive regulatory T cells. Specifically, the drug increased precentage of CD25+CD8+ T cells in the lungs and tumor, as well as increased CD25 MFI on lung CD8+ T cells (Fig. 6B). It also reduced Foxp3 MFI in Tregs in the spleen, and marginaly reduced precentage of activated Tregs in the lungs (Fig. 6C). Assessing myeloid cells, Maraviroc reduced PT precentages of M-MDSCs and PT numbers of both M-MDSCs and PMN-MDSCs (Fig. 6D), and increased iNOS MFI in M1-like macrophages in the circulation, as well as marginally increased circulatory M1-like macrophages precentages (Fig. 6E). 
In the B16F10 model, Maraviroc similarly increased precentage of CD8+ T cells (a marginal effect), and reduced circulatory Tregs precentages as well as tumor Treg numbers (Supplementary Fig. S5A-B). Maraviroc also marginaly reduced tumor M-MDSCs numbers and their expression of iNOS (Supplementary Fig. S5C) and increased iNOS MFI in M1-like macrophages (Supplementary Fig. S5D).
Perioperative blockade of CXCR3 with AMG 487 in the 4T1 model marginaly reduced tumor CD25+ MFI on CD8+ Tcells and precentages of circulatory CD8+ Tcells (Fig. 6F). The drug also reduced lung and spleen PMN-MDSCs precentages, as well as spleen iNOS MFI in PMN-MDSCs (Fig. 6G). Last, AMG 487 also increased spleen proportion of M1-like macrophages (Fig. 6H).
In the B16F10 model, AMG 487 also marginally increased CD25 MFI in lung CD8+ Tcells and marginally reduced tumor Treg precentages (Supplementary Fig. S5E-F). Similarly to the 4T1 model, AMG 487 also reduced iNOS MFI in tumor M-MDSCs and reduced spleen M-MDSCs numbers (Supplementary Fig. S5G).

Last, to explore the relation between perioperative tumor secretion and the metastatic outcome, we utilized the 4T1 model to assess correlations between PT secretion levels of CCL5 and CXCL10 at PT excision, and long-term survival. We found that higher PT secretion levels of both CCL5 and CXCL10 correlated with reduced survival (Fig. 7A-B), and PT CCL5 levels also positively correlated with lungs weight at the survival endpoint, indicating higher metastatic load (Fig. 7C). Similarly, in a cohort of breast cancer patients, higher tumor expression levels of CXCL10 correlated with reduced recurrence-free survival (Fig. 7D), supporting the prognostic significance of perioperative levels of CXCL10.

Materials and Methods

Mice
Male and female C57BL/6J mice, and female BALB/c mice, 8-15 weeks old, were housed 3-5 per cage at 22±1ºC with ad-libitum access to food and water on a 12:12 light-dark cycle. Housing conditions and all experiments involving animals were monitored and approved by the Tel Aviv University Institutional Animal Care and Use Committee (IACUC). Age, weight, sex and order of drug administration were counterbalanced between experimental groups in all experiment. Approval numbers below under Ethics statement. 

Cancer and fibroblast cells
4T1, B16F10, B16F1, and 3T3 NIH fibroblast cell lines were received from Prof. Yuval Shaked. Cells were cultured with RPMI 1640 medium supplemented with 10% FCS, 1% penicillin-streptomycin, 2 mM l-glutamine, 1 mM sodium pyruvate, and 0.1 mM non-essential amino acids. Cell cultures were maintained at 37 °C with 5% CO2.

Tumor cell inoculation
Cells were removed from the culture flask with a 0.25% trypsin-EDTA solution (Sigma-Aldrich) in PBS, washed once in PBS (500g for 10 min) and adjusted to a final concentration in PBS containing 0.1 mg/ml BSA. In the B16F10 model, 200,000 cells in 20ul were inoculated to the footpad, except for flow cytometry experiments in which 250,000 cells in 50ul were inoculated to the left flank. In the 4T1 model, 100,000 cells in 20ul were inoculated to the left 4th mammary fat pad. Cells were kept on ice during the entire injection procedure, which was performed under 2% isoflurane anesthesia. More than 95% of cells were vital throughout the injection period.

Tumor growth and excision 
Primary tumors (PTs) were measured 3 times per week, starting on day 10 from tumor cell inoculation. Tumors were measured by a caliper, and volume was calculated by the formula: (length × width2) × 0.5. In the B16F10 model, tumors were excised when reaching 100mm3 volume (20-40 days following tumor inoculation), except for flow cytometry experiments in which tumors were excised on day 14 following inoculation, reaching an average PT volume of 171 mm3. In the 4T1 model, tumors were excised 14 days following tumor cell inoculation, with an average tumor volume of 85mm3, except flow cytometry experiments in which tumors were excised on day 21 following inoculation, reaching an average PT volume of 300 mm3. Animals were divided into experimental conditions counterbalancing PT volume according to the most recent measurement. When assessing metastatic endpoint in the 4T1 model, a short and minimally invasive procedure was performed to remove the PT. Specifically, PT resection was performed under 2% isoflurane anesthesia, lasting less than 5 minutes. A small aseptic incision in the skin near the tumor was performed using surgical scissors, without injuring the peritoneal cavity, the PT was excised, and the skin was immediately sutured. Secretome sample preparation – tumor cell line and excised tumor CM
Tumor (PT or metastases) was excised without interrupting the tumor capsule, weighted, and placed in a 6-well plate under sterile conditions. Tumor mass was cut into quarters using a scalpel, and 1200-2000ul (according to tumor weight) of serum-free media (SFM) was added for 4-18 hours incubation, depending on the experiment. Following incubation, medium was collected from well and centrifuged (1600rpm, 5min, 4oC) to discard cell debris, and supernatant was collected, aliquoted and kept in -80oC until use. To produce metastatic secretome, 1-4 metastases per animal (from different organs) were harvested and separately incubated in SFM for 6 hours. 

Metastasis follow up and quantification – survival B16 and F10 vs F1 calibration
In the 4T1 model, metastatic follow-up was conducted until a predetermined deadline was reached, defined as 70 days from PT excision. Mice that showed signs of metastatic disease before this deadline were sacrificed for enumeration of metastases. Signs included 20% overall or 10% daily weight loss, rapid shallow breathing, non-responsiveness to cage agitation, or limb weakness or paralysis. Upon reaching criteria, animals were weighed, lung, liver, and spleen were harvested and weighed, followed by lung fixation for 24 hours in Bouin’s solution (72% picric acid, 23% formaldehyde (4% solution), and 5% glacial acetic acid) for metastases count. Lung surface metastases were defined as previously described54. In short, metastases were defined as visible, above 1mm3 in volume, mushroom-shaped, distinctly separated white masses raising above the lung surface. Other organs including adrenal glands, kidneys, intestines, chest cavity (including the rib cage, sternum and spine), thymus, heart, bones and lymph nodes were also assessed for metastases, with the same criteria for metastases assessment as the lungs. Metastatic load score was defined as the sum of organs presenting with overt metastases. All metastases were quantified by an experimenter blinded to the experimental conditions. The metastatic load score, the number of lung metastases, and lung weight values for each animal were normalized to the time elapsed between tumor excision and animal sacrifice. 

Proliferation assay 
4T1, B16F10 or B16F1 cancer cells, or 3T3 NIH fibroblasts were cultured in growth media, washed and incubated with CM or SM in 96-well plates in three cell concentrations - 5,000 , 10,000 or 20,000 cells in 100ul medium per well. Proliferation was measured using Cell Proliferation Reagent WST-1 (Sigma-Aldrich) according to the manufacturer instructions, with a minimum of triplicates per condition. Following 4 and 20 hours of incubation, WST-1 was added to each well for 2 hours before assessment of absorbance intensity. The absorbance intensity of the baseline read immediately following the addition of the WST-1 reagent was subtracted from the 2 hours incubation read with the WST-1 reagent.

Blood draw and plasma collection
Mice were euthanized with isoflurane, and blood was drawn from the heart, within less than 3 min of approaching the animals’ cage, using EDTA-containing syringes (1.8 mg/1ml blood). Blood was then centrifuged for 20 min at 2000g, 4°C for plasma separation, which was collected and stored at -80°C until assayed.

Mass spectrometry-based secretome analysis 
Secretome samples were centrifuged to eliminate cell debris and in-vitro samples also went through 0.45µm filtration and concentration with 3 kDa Amicon filters. Each secretome sample was mixed at a 1:1 ratio with 8M urea in 100 mM Tris buffer (pH 8.5). Before protein digestion, proteins from the samples were incubated with 1mM Dithiothreitol followed by 5mM Iodoacetamide. Urea concentration was diluted to 2M by adding 50 mm ammonium bicarbonate, followed by overnight digestion with LysC/Trypsin mix (Promega) and sequencing-grade modified Trypsin (Promega) at room temperature. Peptides were then acidified with 1ul of Trifluoroacetic acid and samples were desalted and concentrated on C18 StageTips55. Prior to MS analysis, peptides were eluted from StageTips using 80% acetonitrile, vacuum-concentrated and diluted in MS loading buffer (2% acetonitrile, 0.1% formic acid). Liquid Chromatography-Mass Spectrometry (LC-MS) was performed using the nano-ultra high-performance liquid chromatography system (UHPLC) (Easy-nLC1000, Thermo Fisher Scientific), followed by MS analysis on the Q-Exactive Plus and Q-Exactive HF mass spectrometers (Thermo Fisher Scientific). Peptides were separated by reverse-phase chromatography (50 cm long EASY-Spray PepMap columns; Thermo Fisher Scientific) with a 140 min linear gradient of water/acetonitrile. 

Proteomics data analysis 
Mass spectrometry (MS) raw files were analyzed by MaxQuant56, and the Label-free quantification algorithm57. MS/MS spectra were referenced to the Uniprot mouse proteome by the Andromeda search engine58. A false discovery rate (FDR) of 0.01 was used on both the peptide and protein levels based on a decoy database. Statistical analysis was performed using the Perseus software59, String database (www.string-db.org), Cytoscape software60 and R environment61. Biological annotations were taken from Gene Ontology (GO)62.

Tissue processing for flow cytometry (FACS)
Twelve hours following laparotomy, animals were sacrificed by an overdose of isoflurane, and 0.6 ml of blood was withdrawn by cardiac puncture into syringes containing EDTA (1.8 mg/ml blood; Sigma-Aldrich). Following blood withdrawal, spleen, lung and PT were harvested, washed in PBS, dried and weighted, and placed in PBS on ice. Single cell suspensions were prepared from each harvested organ according to the following specifications: Organ was minced with surgical scissors into 2-3 mm pieces, on ice, then dissociated (only lungs and PTs) in serum-free RPMI medium (biological industries, Israel) containing 160 units/ml collagenase 4 (Worthington #LS004189), and 100 units/ml DNAse (Sigma # D5025-150KU), in a volume of 2cc per lung or per 100mg tumor mass, for 40 min incubation in 37oC shaking incubator at 70 rpm. 20cc 10% FCS RPMI medium was added per sample prior to passage through a 70uM nylon cell strainer (corning). Samples were then centrifuged (400g 5min 4oC), and supernatant was removed. Spleens did not undergo the enzymatic dissociation step, and were mechanically disrupted and directly passed two passages through cell strainer, first at 70uM and second at 40uM (corning). Red blood cells (RBC) were lysed in all organs using 5cc lysis buffer (0.8g NH4Cl, 0.1g KHCO3 in 100 ml DDW (Merck)) per sample, for 5 min incubation in room temperature (RT). Blood samples underwent RBC lysis twice, the first time with 1:10 lysis buffer volume relative to blood volume, and second time at 5cc lysis buffer volume as all other organs. Cells were then washed with 30cc cold PBS, centrifuged (400g 5min 4oC), divided into aliquots and underwent direct staining and FACS, and/or gradual freezing in freezing medium (90% FCS+ 10% DMSO). In the B16F10 model, PT and lungs were used fresh for FACS, and blood and spleen were first frozen and later thawed for FACS. In the 4T1 model, all organ samples were first frozen. All frozen samples were kept in -80oC freezer until use. 

Flow cytometry (FACS)
Flow cytometry was used to assess the percentages and numbers of several immune cell populations, including Myeloid (M)-MDSCs, Polymorphonuclear (PMN)-MDSCs, CD4+ T cells, and CD8+ T cells. In the B1F10 model, regulatory T cells and macrophages were also studied. Immune populations were defined as described in Supplementary Fig. S6.
The following antibodies were used: anti-mouse CD16/CD32 (clone S17011E, dilution 1:200), anti-mouse CD3-PE (clone 17A2, dilution 1:160), anti-mouse CD4-PerCP-Cy5.5 (clone RM4-4, dilution 1:320), anti-mouse CD8-APC (clone 53-6.7, dilution 1:640), anti-mouse CD45-FITC (clone 30-F11, dilution 1:800), anti-CD11B-PerCP-Cy5.5 (clone M1/70, dilution 1:320), anti-mouse Ly6C-PE (clone HK1.4, dilution 1:160), and anti-mouse Ly6G-APC/Fire (clone 1A8, dilution 1:160).  
The following antibodies were used in the B16F10 model: anti-mouse CD16/CD32 (clone S17011E, dilution 1:200), anti-mouse CD45-FITC (clone 30-F11, dilution 1:800), anti-CD11B-PerCP-Cy5.5 (clone M1/70, dilution 1:320), anti-mouse Ly6C-PE (clone HK1.4, dilution 1:160), anti-mouse Ly6G-APC/Fire (clone 1A8, dilution 1:160),  anti-mouse CD3-APC/Fire (clone 17A2, dilution 1:80),  anti-mouse CD4-PerCP-Cy5.5 (clone RM4-4, dilution 1:320), anti-mouse CD8-APC (clone 53-6.7, dilution 1:640), anti-mouse CD25-BV605 (clone A18246A, dilution 1:80), anti-mouse F4-80 (clone BM8, dilution 1:50), anti-mouse iNOS-APC (clone W16030C, dilution 1:160), anti-FOXP3-PE (clone 150D, dilution 1:200), anti-mouse CD206-PE (clone C068C2, dilution 1:80). All antibodies were purchased from Biolegend. 
Zombie violet dye (BioLegend 423113, dilution 1:200) was added together with surface marker staining, according to manufacturer instructions. 

Frozen samples were defrosted for 2 min in 37oC bath, transferred to warm 20cc 10% FCS RPMI medium, centrifuged (400g 5min 4oC) and pellet was suspended in staining buffer (1% FCS in PBS). Cells were then counted and a subsample from each sample was stained with 7AAD (eBioscience) to assess cells viability. 1x106 cells from each sample were then incubated with anti-CD16/32 (Fc receptors blocker, 0.25μg per 106 cells) at 4°C for 10 minutes. Cells were then stained with surface staining antibodies for 20 minutes incubation at 4°C, protected from light, then washed with staining buffer and, if no intra-cellular staining was performed, suspended in FACS buffer (1%FCS and 2mM EDTA in PBS) at concentration of 2M cells/ml. 
For intra-cellular staining, 1x106 cells were surface stained as above, then fixed and permeabilized using the FoxP3/Transcription Factor Staining Buffer Set (eBiosciences) as per the manufacturer’s protocol. Cells were then stained with anti-mouse iNOS-APC (clone W16030C, dilution 1:160), anti-FOXP3-PE (clone 150D, dilution 1:200), and in the lungs also with anti-mouse CD206-PE (clone C068C2, dilution 1:80) from Biolegend, washed and resuspended in FACS buffer as above. 
All data were collected on a Cytoflex S flow cytometer (Beckman Coulter) and analyzed with FlowJo v7 software (Tree Star, Inc.). Absolute numbers of cells in all tissues were calculated by computing the percentage of a specific cell type of the total cell number of a given sample, divided by milligram weight to normalize tissue weight differences.

Statistical analysis
Statistical analyses were performed using GraphPad Prism software and/or STATVIEW software. Following F-test of equality of variances, one- or two-way factorial analyses of variance (ANOVA) were used. Provided significant group differences were found, Fisher’s protected least significant differences (Fisher’s PLSD) contrasts or Tukey test were performed to test pair-wise post hoc comparisons, correcting for multiple comparisons. The significance level in all statistical tests was set at P < 0.05, and all statistical tests were two-sided. Outliers were defined as a single data point per experimental group, which lays more than 2 standard deviations beyond the group’s mean. Simple linear regression and Pearson correlations were used to assess correlations between 2 continuous variables. Animals which did not develop a PT or died for unknown reasons were excluded from all analyses. For experiments involving both sexes, we assessed potential sex interactions for each measured variable. In the absence of significant sex effects or sex-by-group interactions, data from both sexes were combined for analysis. Sample size was determined based on preliminary experiments.
